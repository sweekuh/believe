Belief

The yellow sign over the door

At the very end, Ted tapes a single hand-lettered word above the locker room door, with Keeley’s help: BELIEVE.

There’s a real idea behind it. The philosopher William James called it precursive faith — choosing to believe a good thing is possible before there’s any proof, because the believing itself helps make it come true. It isn’t pretending. It’s deciding to act as if the better outcome is reachable.

That taped-up sign is the whole show in one word. Everything Ted does for three seasons is an argument for it.

William James, “The Will to Believe” (1896). Discussed in Ted Lasso and Philosophy (Wiley, 2024), ch. 3.

Seeing people

He learns the kit man’s name

Ted greets Nate, the equipment manager everyone else walks straight past, by name — and treats him like he matters.

A tiny moment carrying a big idea: that every person is owed real attention regardless of their rank. Kant called it treating people as ends in themselves, never as just tools to get something done.

Keep this one in your back pocket. Where Nate starts in this scene, and where he ends up by Season 3, is one of the show’s biggest journeys.

Respect for persons (Kantian ethics).

Hope vs. naivety

Walking into a hostile room with a smile

Ted faces a press conference full of people who think he’s a joke. He knows almost nothing about the sport, and instead of bluffing, he just stays open and warm.

The show is quietly asking a hard question here: is that kind of relentless optimism just foolish, or is it a discipline? One answer comes from Camus, who argued for a clear-eyed hope — you see exactly how bad things are, and you choose to hope anyway. Not blindness. A decision.

Watch across the season for whether Ted’s cheerfulness is real strength or a way of hiding from his own pain. The show doesn’t let him off easy.

Albert Camus on hope and the absurd. Explored in Ted Lasso and Philosophy (Wiley, 2024), ch. 15.

Revenge or justice

Why Rebecca really hired him

After defending Ted in public, Rebecca admits in private that she hired him hoping he’ll fail — to ruin the one thing her ex-husband loves.

This is one of the oldest questions in ethics: is revenge ever just? Rebecca feels she’s owed it. The series slowly draws a line between wanting someone to suffer and wanting a wrong made right — and they turn out to be very different things.

Rebecca is the show’s deepest redemption story. This scene is her at her lowest, on purpose, so you can see how far she travels.

Revenge vs. justice; see Ted Lasso and Philosophy (Wiley, 2024), ch. 20.

Small kindnesses

Biscuits with the Boss

Ted starts bringing Rebecca a little pink box of biscuits every single morning, a ritual he names “Biscuits with the Boss.”

It looks like nothing. But it’s an argument the whole show believes in: that trust gets built through small, consistent, unconditional gestures, not grand ones — and he keeps doing it for someone who is secretly working against him.

That little box is the thread that eventually unravels her whole plan. (And keep watching — you find out he bakes them himself.)
